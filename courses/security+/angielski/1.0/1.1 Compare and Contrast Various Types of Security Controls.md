
Security controls are safeguards or countermeasures designed to protect systems, data, and physical assets from threats. They help reduce the risk of security incidents by either preventing them, detecting them, or minimizing their impact.

These controls are a fundamental part of any organization’s cybersecurity strategy and are essential for maintaining the confidentiality, integrity, and availability (CIA) of information systems.

CompTIA Security+ emphasizes real-world cybersecurity concepts. Understanding security controls is critical because:

- they form the foundation of risk management,
    
- they are used to protect systems against evolving threats,
    
- they help ensure compliance with laws, regulations, and standards,
    
- they enable layered security, also known as defense in depth,
    
- they prepare professionals to evaluate and implement secure environments.

# Categories of Security Controls

## Technical Controls

Technical controls are implemented using technology to protect systems and data.

If it is code, software, or a system configuration that enforces security, it is technical.

Examples:

- firewalls,
    
- encryption,
    
- multi-factor authentication,
    
- intrusion detection systems,
    
- antivirus and anti-malware software.
    

---

## Managerial Controls

Managerial controls are policies, procedures, and documentation that guide how security is managed.

If it is written down in a policy, rule, or plan and guides behavior or decisions, it is managerial.

Examples:

- risk assessments,
    
- security policies,
    
- vendor management procedures,
    
- incident response plans,
    
- data classification policies.
    

---

## Operational Controls

Operational controls are day-to-day procedures and practices used to maintain and enforce security.

If people are doing it as part of their job to keep systems secure, it is operational.

Examples:

- security awareness training,
    
- change management,
    
- incident handling processes,
    
- account reviews,
    
- user access reviews.
    

---

## Physical Controls

Physical controls are physical measures used to protect assets, facilities, and personnel.

If you can touch it or it physically stops someone, it is physical.

Examples:

- locked doors,
    
- security guards,
    
- surveillance cameras,
    
- fencing,
    
- biometric access to server rooms.

# Key Areas of Managing Security Controls

Effective security depends not only on implementing controls, but also on managing them properly.

## Control Selection

- Based on risk assessments and business needs.
    
- Choose controls appropriate to the environment, such as cloud, on-premises, or hybrid.
    

## Implementation

- Ensure controls are properly configured and integrated.
    
- Document the deployment process for repeatability and audits.
    

## Monitoring and Testing

- Use continuous monitoring tools such as SIEM, IDS/IPS, and vulnerability scanners.
    
- Perform regular audits and penetration testing.
    

## Review and Maintenance

- Regularly evaluate control effectiveness.
    
- Update controls based on new threats or system changes.
    

## Policy Alignment

- Controls must align with organizational security policies and compliance frameworks, such as NIST, ISO 27001, and CIS.
    
- Not all security controls are the same. They may vary depending on the company or country.
    

> Security controls are not exclusive to a single category.

# Control Types

## Preventive Controls

Preventive controls stop security incidents before they happen. They block access to a resource.

> “You shall not pass!”

|Technical|Managerial|Operational|Physical|
|---|---|---|---|
|Firewalls|Acceptable Use Policy|Security awareness training|Locked server racks|
|Multi-factor authentication|Security baseline standards|Background checks|Fences around perimeter|
|Email filters|Vendor access policy|Regular access reviews|ID badges|
|Secure system configuration|Account creation policy|Change control process|Biometrics|
|Data Loss Prevention|Secure development guidelines|Guard shack|Guard shack|

---

## Deterrent Controls

Deterrent controls discourage an attacker or unauthorized user from attempting a security violation.

They do not directly prevent access.

|Technical|Managerial|Operational|Physical|
|---|---|---|---|
|Login barriers|Policy on disciplinary action for violations|Security awareness posters|Surveillance cameras|
|IDS with visible monitoring|HR policies outlining consequences|Regular compliance training|“This area is under surveillance” signs|
|Account lockout after failed attempts|Clear BYOD guidelines|Visible security walkthroughs|Guard dogs|
|Monitoring alerts visible to users|Legal disclaimers|Policy acknowledgment forms|Visible security cameras|
|Warning messages in scripts|Compliance warning policies|Warning emails about policy enforcement|Do-not-enter signs|
|DLP log and alert|||Security lights|

---

## Detective Controls

Detective controls identify and report unauthorized or abnormal activity after it occurs or while it is happening.

They may not prevent access.

|Technical|Managerial|Operational|Physical|
|---|---|---|---|
|Intrusion Detection Systems|Audit log reviews|Manual log review|Surveillance cameras|
|SIEM alerts|Internal audits|Incident response team investigation|Motion detectors|
|Antivirus logs|Forensics plans|Regular security walkthroughs|Alarm systems|
|Log monitoring tools|Security assessments|User access reviews|Door open/close sensor logs|
|File integrity checkers|Compliance tracking policies|Alert monitoring procedures|Badge scan logs|

---

## Corrective Controls

Corrective controls fix the issue after a security event, restore systems to normal operation, or allow systems to continue operating with minimal downtime.

|Technical|Managerial|Operational|Physical|
|---|---|---|---|
|Patching software vulnerabilities|Creating or updating policies for reporting security issues|Performing root cause analysis|Surveillance cameras used post-incident for investigation and improvement|
|Removing malware|Revised risk assessments|Implementing new SOPs|Replacing damaged security equipment|
|Reimaging infected systems|Lessons learned meetings|Updating the incident response plan|Installing improved locks|
|Restoring from backup|Incident Response Plan|Revising training procedures|Upgrading alarm systems|
|System rollback tools|New vendor requirements|Changing access roles|Reinforcing walls or doors|

---

## Compensating Controls

Compensating controls are alternative controls that provide similar protection when the primary control is not possible or practical.

They may exist only temporarily.

|Technical|Managerial|Operational|Physical|
|---|---|---|---|
|Token-based authentication|Documented exception with extra monitoring|Manual logging instead of automated SIEM|Security guard in place of a broken door lock|
|Host-based firewall when no perimeter firewall is available|Temporary acceptance of risk with signed approval|Increased audits|Temporary barriers|
|Disk encryption when full device encryption is not supported|Alternative vendor review process|Manual alerts in place of automation|Manual visitor logs|
|VPN when network segmentation is not feasible|Alternative vendor review process|Temporary account reviews|Extra patrols during system outage|
|Application whitelisting|Interim policy procedures|Offline backup verification||

---

## Directive Controls

Directive controls provide guidance or instruction to influence behavior.

This is a relatively weak security control because it is not based on enforcement, but on requesting or instructing users to act in a certain way.

|Technical|Managerial|Operational|Physical|
|---|---|---|---|
|System-enforced password policies|Security baseline standards|Security awareness training|Exit route maps|
|Login banners with rules|Incident Response Plan|Security drills|Evacuation signage|
|Configured timeouts for inactivity|Code of conduct|Instructions for secure document disposal|Access procedure signs|
|Software wizards that guide secure setup|Data handling standards|Daily standups with reminders|Safety instructions near equipment|
|Built-in security tutorials|Business continuity plans|||
